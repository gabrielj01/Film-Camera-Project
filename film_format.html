Film Formats

Film, also called film stock, is a crucial element in analog photography.
Here is a detailed explanation of the main aspects related to film film formats:

  2. ISO Sensibility

Common size

Photographic film is available in several sizes, the most common being:
35mm: The most popular format, used in most 35mm cameras.
120mm: Also known as medium format, used in medium format cameras.

  1. 35mm

    2. This format is the most common. The film is typically 35mm wide and is loaded in cartridges that make it easy to load into the camera.
    It produces standard 24mm x 36mm rectangular images. 35mm films are available in various sensitivities (ISO), suitable for different lighting conditions.

  2. 120 mm

    3. Used in medium format cameras, this format offers superior image quality due to its larger size compared to 35mm.
    The images produced have varying dimensions depending on the camera,
    generally around 6 cm x 6 cm or 6 cm x 9 cm. This format is popular for artistic and professional photography.

ISO Sensibility

Film films have different ISO sensitivities to accommodate varying light conditions.
Lower sensitivity films (ISO 100-200) are suitable for daylight, while higher sensitivity films (ISO 800 and above) are suitable for low light conditions.

Differents type of films

Black and White: Offers a timeless aesthetic, ideal for artistic expression.
Color: Available in various palettes, each offering distinct tones.
Slides: Produces positive transparencies for direct projection.

Special formats

Some films offer special formats, such as panoramic format or half format, allowing for increased creativity.
These formats are suitable for professional and high-quality applications.

Storage and Preserve

Silver films are sensitive to heat, humidity and light. Storage in cool, dry conditions is crucial to maintaining their quality.
To preserve a film, it is recommended to clean it regularly and store it in airtight containers.

Development and Printing

After shooting, film film requires a process of developing and printing to obtain final images.
This can be done professionally or at home for experienced amateurs.
This process includes development, stopping, fixing, and washing.
